The Impact of Gender on Mouse Activity Levels

Day

By: Ritvik Mohindru, Brooklyn Pagaza, Sai Sri Lasya Yadlapati
WRITEUP
The interactive visualization we made answers the following question: How do daily and hourly activity levels vary by male and female mice? Understanding gender differences in mouse activity will help us learn how hormones and biological factors shape behavior, which is critical for research on health and treatments. This exploration helps answer how hormones and gender can affect behavior and how mice can be used to study human health conditions.

Our visualization compares the activity levels of male and female mice, illustrating both the daily and hourly fluctuations. It also highlights the estrus periods during the 14 days, in order to explore how reproductive cycles or hormonal changes can influence activity levels of female mice.

The visualization we made is two-part: it includes a main, bigger plot that illustrates the daily trends of activity levels of male and female mice, and the smaller plot to the bottom right that illustrates the hourly activity level fluctuations of male and female mice. There is a slider below the smaller plot that allows the viewer to interact with the visualization and control the day they wish to observe. The data we used to create this visualization came from the provided mouse dataset. In order to generate the data values used in the big and small line plots, we wrangled the data by averaging the activity levels for male and female mice per day and per hour.

Our interactive visualization uses numerous visual encoding techniques to present the data in a clear, evocative, and effective way. The first set of interactive features we incorporated were regarding hovering over the line plots. Hovering over any given day/hour will result in the points for the corresponding day/hour being larger in size, a dashed line going through the points marking the day/hour, and a tooltip that would display the day/hour and its specific corresponding male and female activity levels next to the cursor. The reasoning behind these visual encodings is simple: the larger points would make it clear which points the viewer's cursor is currently over, the dashed line makes it clear the corresponding day/hour, and the tooltip gives the specific values for male and female activity levels for the viewers use. In addition to hovering, another interactive element we incorporated was a slider element below the smaller plot, which would show the hourly activity levels for mice. Adjusting the slider would adjust the day for which the graph would be shown for. This would also adjust the corresponding highlight on the larger graph, which shows what day is being looked at. The highlight is red for days of estrus and yellow for days without estrus. The reason these colors were chosen is because red represents something characteristic of a female, as estrus is, while yellow-orange would do well as a mild color to represent a normal day. Likewise, the colors chosen for the graph were blue and pink to intuitively represent the male and female genders. Furthermore, because the line graphs are set on the same scale and represent “Activity Level,” it employs a position on a common scale visual channel. This makes the visualization expressive and allows the viewer to quickly compare the activity levels between males and females, on both the plots. Lastly, the interactive slider enables the viewer to obtain more data and information regarding the question, as it's aggregated by the hour, instead of by each day.

Some alternatives we considered include a scatter plot instead of a line plot, and using raw data instead of aggregating it by hour or by day. We eliminated these ideas because they would be too overwhelming for the viewer to digest too much data at once. We chose to do a two-plot layout so that viewers can visualize both the daily average activity levels and also the hourly fluctuations side-by-side. We chose line plots because they make it easy to track and compare how activity levels change over time for both male and female mice. Daily and hourly views can provide a more complete picture — daily shows the overall trends and while hourly captures the smaller fluctuations, like their nocturnal activity.

Through these interactive elements, the viewer should be able to explore and notice interesting patterns. For example, through the red highlight marking days of estrus, we can notice that females have a spike in activity levels, and relatively higher activity levels compared to male mice. Moreover, the hourly activity levels as depicted in the small interactive slider plot illustrates how mice are more active during early hours of the day because they are nocturnal.
